Family demands accountability after their 4-year-old daughter drowns at a Miami Airbnb

Kennedi Ray died on March 5, 2026 in a pool with no safety barriers. Her parents are suing Airbnb and demanding enforcement of Florida's Residential Pool Safety Law.

Sources: NBC6 Miami · New York Post Published: March 24–25, 2026

On March 5, 2026, Kennedi Ray, a 4-year-old girl diagnosed with autism, was found at the bottom of the pool at a property rented through Airbnb on the 20200 block of Northeast 15th Avenue, in northeast Miami-Dade County. The family, originally from Atlanta, Georgia, was staying at the property when the tragedy occurred. Her parents were asleep when the little girl went out to the backyard on her own and fell into the pool. No alarm sounded. There were no barriers. No safety device stopped her.

"I lost my daughter. I lost my 4-year-old baby girl. She only had four years here. We loved her so much. We miss her so much. I'm never going to hear my daughter laugh again. I just want to hold her."

— Adenah Francis, Kennedi Ray's mother

On Tuesday, March 24, the family held an emotional press conference at the very location where the tragedy occurred. Attorney Adam Finkel of Haggard Law Firm was emphatic in pointing out the complete absence of mandatory safety measures at the property.

⚖️ What Was Allegedly Missing from the Property

  • No safety barrier or perimeter fence around the pool
  • No alarm on the backyard access door (sliding door)
  • No safety cover for the pool
  • No door lock on the pool area access
  • The property was listed as "family-friendly" on Airbnb

Attorney Finkel stated that the property violated the Miami-Dade County vacation rental ordinance. "A child had access to the backyard. No alarm went off, there were no barriers, and we lost Kennedi. How can a house be advertised as family-friendly when it has a pool and none of the safety measures are in effect?" The family plans to file a formal lawsuit against Airbnb and demands that the property be removed from all rental platforms.

📣 Official Statement from Airbnb

"The loss of a child is a tragedy and our hearts go out to the Ray family. Airbnb has supported recent bipartisan efforts in the Florida state legislature to strengthen safety standards across all homes with pools. We also continue to work with partners on education campaigns and initiatives to promote water safety, including discounted pool fences for hosts and in-app safety tips for guests booking near water."

This case adds to a documented trend in Florida: short-term rental properties with pools pose a disproportionate risk when the legally required safety measures are not implemented. Florida's Residential Swimming Pool Safety Act (Florida Statute 515.23) was created to prevent this type of tragedy, requiring barriers, fences, and self-closing gates. However, enforcement at vacation rental properties remains inconsistent.

The law requires that pools have at least one of these barriers: perimeter fence with self-closing gate, approved cover, ASTM F2208 certified pool alarm, or UL 2017 alarm on all access doors. For vacation rental properties in Miami-Dade, all applicable measures are mandatory.
